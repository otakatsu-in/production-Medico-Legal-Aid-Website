# The Doctor's Guide to Legally Sound Consent in India

Consent is the most frequently litigated aspect of medical negligence in India. In over 60% of cases filed before consumer forums, the patient alleges that "informed consent was not taken" or that "risks were not explained."

To protect your practice, you must understand what constitutes legally valid consent under Indian law.

---

### The Evolution: Prior Informed Consent

Historically, simple "implied consent" (the patient lying down on the examination table) or "blanket consent" was considered sufficient. Today, the courts demand **Prior Informed Consent**.

This means:
1. **Prior:** Consent must be taken well before the procedure, when the patient is in a calm, un-sedated, and clear frame of mind. Consent taken inside the operation theater or 10 minutes before surgery is often declared invalid by courts.
2. **Informed:** The doctor must explain the nature of the procedure, risks, complications, alternatives, and expected success rates in a language the patient understands.

---

### The Three Golden Rules of Consent

According to landmark Supreme Court judgments, particularly *Samira Kohli vs. Dr. Prabha Manchanda*, there are three absolute rules you must follow:

#### Rule 1: Proxy Consent is Not Valid for Adults
If the patient is a competent adult (18 years or older), only **they** can sign the consent form. A husband's signature is not valid for a wife, and a father's signature is not valid for an adult son.
*Exception:* If the patient is unconscious, mentally incapacitated, or in a life-threatening emergency, proxy consent is allowed.

#### Rule 2: Separate Consent for Separate Procedures
Consent for a diagnostic procedure does not authorize a therapeutic procedure. For example, if you take consent for a diagnostic laparoscopy and find an ovarian cyst, you cannot perform a cystectomy under the same anesthesia unless it is a life-saving emergency. You must wait, wake the patient up, explain the findings, and take fresh consent.

#### Rule 3: The Witness is Mandatory
A consent form must always be witnessed. A witness is a third party (a nurse, a relative, or another hospital staff member) who signs to confirm that the explanation was given and the patient signed voluntarily.

---

### Best Practices for Your Clinic

To ensure your consent forms are legally airtight, implement these steps today:

- **Use Local Languages:** If your patient speaks Kannada, Hindi, or Tamil, the consent form must be in that language. If using an English form, write a note stating: *"The procedure and risks have been explained to me in [Language] and I have understood them."*
- **Add a Handwritten Line:** Ask the patient to write in their own handwriting: *"I have understood the risks and I consent to the surgery."* This makes it impossible for them to claim in court that they signed without reading.
- **Document the Timing:** Always write the exact date and time when the consent was explained and signed.
- **Separate Anaesthesia Consent:** Anaesthesia carries its own set of risks. The anaesthetist must explain these risks and obtain a separate consent form. The surgeon cannot take anaesthesia consent.

Remember, a legally sound consent form is your strongest shield in a courtroom. Uphold documentation discipline and protect your medical career.
